I love mechanical watches. Despite being significantly less accurate than pretty much every other watch technology since the 1950s (like quartz), there’s something beautiful and elegant in the tiny gears and springs of mechanical movements, which also never need batteries.

My first mechanical watch is an automatic: a Hamilton Khaki Navy GMT that uses a Swiss-made self-winding movement. When I sought to add another watch to the collection, I figured a manual winding watch would be more convenient since I wouldn’t have to worry about wearing it every day (or investing in an electric winder, which for mechanical watches seems a bit of a silly idea) just to keep it wound.

I’m drawn to pilot watches because of the legibility of the dial, which traditionally are large black and black and sported heavy white sans-serif numerals — originally designed to be readable in the poor lighting of World War-II era aircraft, and to be worn over thick bomber jackets. The end result has been an extremely readable design that persists today. In making my rounds of watch forums and blogs, I came across Chinese watchmakers like Parnis making beautiful classically styled watches for cheap.

This is actually my second Chinese hand-wound watch. I previously owned a Garton pilot watch that I also really loved. But one day, I made the poor decision of letting it get near-soaking wet. The dial casement popped off not long after, and then the movement just plain stopped working. I could have gotten it repaired, but it would have costed better than 50% of the original sub-$100 cost.

I learned a few things from the Garton about hand-wound watches. Firstly, that winding the big, solitary crown of a hand-wound movement is kind of therapeutic to me. I rarely let it wind down to a stop. Second, these pilot watches are so readable, and so much lighter than the Hamilton (which, to be fair, is all stainless steel) that I ended up wearing them more frequently than the Hamilton. Third, the Garton had excellent lume that kept it readable even through a movie. It didn’t take very long before I yearned to replace it.

Finally, months after it broke, I ordered this 44mm Parnis pilot watch from Manbushijie.com, which forums heavily endorsed as one of the most reliable Chinese watch sources. In about three weeks, I had received it.
