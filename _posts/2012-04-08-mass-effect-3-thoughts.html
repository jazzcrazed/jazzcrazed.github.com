Being a big fan of the first two games, I anticipated Mass Effect 3 about as much as the whole of the gaming world did. I just beat it a couple of weeks ago, and it left a somewhat unsatisfying taste in my mouth.

For those who are new to the series, Mass Effect is a trilogy of action RPGs following the exploits of Commander Shepard as he attempts to save sentient life across the galaxy from an ancient robotic race called the Reapers. It’s a space opera with immense production values, setting a high bar with graphics, acting, and cinematic atmosphere — all the more impressive being a game with a branching storyline and a variety of possible outcomes. But the third game never quite escapes the confines of its mechanic to tell its story compellingly, making the melodrama fall flat.

Most of the problem, I feel, lies in its attempts to flesh out a realistic world and galaxy, and not quite hitting the mark. Suspending disbelief is a huge challenge in games, but there are some sore spots in Mass Effect 3 that could have been avoided simply by not being there.

Homogeneity in Gaming Worlds

Open world game design has been big in the gaming industry for years now, since computing power has improved enough to accommodate its complexities. The trick to an open world game is that it shouldn’t feel like a chore to travel through parts of the world that are irrelevant to game progress in order to get to parts that are relevant.

A common way about this is to pepper the game world with side quests that add relevance to the irrelevance. The Mass Effect games tack this way, with mixed results. In Mass Effect 3, you encounter side quests mostly by wandering through the game world, overhearing side conversations, and listening through to their end. Unfortunately, without variety, this can quickly feel more like a chore than a nice (if not completely necessary to progress) atmospheric detail — “grindy” in RPG parlance.

The process in almost every single case goes like this: you run your character around for several uneventful minutes, waiting for a voice way louder than the background din (and/or for a voice that is accompanied by subtitles, if you have them on) to come within earshot. Then, you stand there, waiting, paying attention to the details of the conversation or not, until the telltale UI cue pops up indicating that a quest has been added to your mission log.

The first problem comes in the unrealism of the situation. I understand the need to easily be able to pick these quests out from just the normal noise of the game world. But having it be the sole voice in a crowd of interacting but muted non-player characters is kind of a cop-out. There may as well just have been a flag waving over the relevant characters’ heads. Or, just remove all the surrounding muted, dumb, prop npcs. The conceit is betrayed by the contrast between the relevant and irrelevant, and more time should have been spent in making the quest acquisition more subtle.

The second part of the problem comes with the quests themselves. They are “fetch quests” for which the resolution is to get in your spaceship, fly around the galaxy, find the necessary object, and return it to the same npc that you overheard needs it.

Flying around Mass Effect 3 means entering a map view and moving a little toy-like representation of your ship, the Normandy SR-2, around a hugely out of scale representation of star systems and the galaxy as a whole. To start, this is a moronic way to navigate. In today’s day and age, we send our spaceships about the solar system programmatically. Thanks to a solid understanding of orbital mechanics, we knew, for example, when and where Titan would be in July of 2004 nearly seven years in advance. We programmed Cassini-Huygens with some telemetry, aimed, fired, and pretty much forgot — at least in the sense of necessary real-time navigation.

Mass Effect takes place in our distant future, long after Huygens poked around the surface of Titan. Not only have we fully mapped our Solar System, but we’ve clearly well-mapped dozens of other solar systems across the Milky Way. Going to Planet X in Star System Y should be a matter of picking its name from a car-GPS-like computer (autocompletion of form inputs included), and then you’re there. Instead, it’s a matter of moving a cursor around arbitrary nodes in a toy Milky Way until the name you want shows up in a popup — stupid already because now I need to hunt for the desired star system. At least printed maps have the advantage of always showing the names of streets and places all of the time.

Once you’ve found the desired system, you enter it with a push of a button, and then you’re plunged (after a pretty but repetitive “jump” cinematic) into an even sillier “zoom level” — a map of the individual solar system where the “mass relay&lrdquo; that facilitates faster-than-light travel is orbiting. Various space errata in the forms of planets, moons, asteroids, and other junk are scattered around this view; planets get the added distinction of having their orbital cycle mapped with a circular line around the star in the center.
