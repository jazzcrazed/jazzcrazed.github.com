--- layout: post title: "My Massive Nitpick of Mass Effect 3" description: Being a big fan of the first two games, I anticipated Mass Effect 3 about as much as the whole of the gaming world did. Despite being a relatively fun experience, navigating around its fictional universe betrays a real lack of experience in interface design. What follows is a long, nerdy nitpick of it. tags: [video games, user interface] date: 2012-04-08 14:15:00 -0400 ---

For those who are new to the series, Mass Effect is a trilogy of action RPGs following the exploits of Commander Shepard as (s)he attempts to save sentient life across the galaxy from an ancient (prehistoric, from a human perspective) robotic race called the Reapers. It’s a space opera with immense production values, setting a high bar with graphics, acting, and cinematic atmosphere — all the more impressive being a game with a branching storyline and a variety of possible outcomes. But the third game never quite escapes the confines of its mechanics to tell its story compellingly, making the melodrama fall flat.

One of its failings is in navigating the fictional Milky Way it’s created, which plays a major role in the core gameplay mechanics. The game formula distills to this: After acquiring a mission, either along the main storyline via cinematics or as side quests acquired from assorted non-player characters, the player travels to the planet or space station where the mission takes place using a navigation computer on the bridge of Shepard’s ship, the Normandy SR-2.

Getting to destinations means entering a map view and moving a little toy-like representation of your ship, around a hugely out of scale representation of star systems and the galaxy as a whole. This is a moronic way to navigate. In today’s day and age, we send our spaceships about the solar system programmatically. Thanks to a solid understanding of orbital mechanics, we knew, for example, when and where Titan would be in July of 2004 nearly seven years in advance. In 1997, we programmed Cassini-Huygens with some telemetry, aimed, fired, and pretty much forgot — at least in the sense of necessary real-time navigation.

Mass Effect takes place in our distant future, long after Huygens poked around the surface of Titan. Not only have we fully mapped our Solar System, but we’ve clearly well-mapped dozens of other solar systems across the Milky Way. Going to Planet X in Star System Y should be a matter of picking its name from a car-GPS-like list (including autocompletion), and then you’re there. Instead, it’s a matter of panning a cursor around arbitrary nodes in a toy Milky Way until the name you want shows up in a popup — stupid already because now I need to hunt for the desired star system. At least printed maps have the advantage of always showing the names of streets and places all of the time.

The next big problem with this view is that there’s no way to zoom the map in or, more importantly, out. This sucks, because the galaxy map doesn’t even fit in a single screen. You have to pan your cursor around to see the edges, where there are indeed things worth paying attention to. It’s a painful victory of form over function, as the vast majority of the Milky Way is just empty, lovely, celestial texture that clearly could have been condensed down into something usable. As annoying as this is here, it’s actually a lot worse in other views, which I’ll get to next.

Once you’ve found the desired system, you enter it with a push of a button, and then you’re plunged (after a lovely but repetitive “jump” cinematic) into an even sillier “zoom level” — a map of the individual solar system where the “mass relay” that facilitates faster-than-light travel is orbiting. Various space errata in the forms of planets, moons, asteroids, and other junk are scattered around this view. Planets get the added distinction of having their orbital cycle mapped with a circular line around the center star(s); though, it’s clearly just for looks, since nothing ever progresses along its orbit (despite the game timeline taking weeks or more).

This is the view where you navigate by pushing your little toy ship around. And, like its bigger galaxy-spanning brother view, nothing is labelled by default. To figure out the name of a planet, station, or other orbiting thing, you have to “fly” your toy Normandy to it. Dumb, because obviously the data is there — why force you to push a toy over it to get that info?

This is made worse by the fact that the toy Normandy for no good reason at all has significant mass, and thus gradual acceleration and momentum. Not because you’re flying through space, mind you, since there’s drag. More as if you had an actual toy on a tabletop that you were nudging around. You can guess how precise this mode of information gathering actually is; a lot of nudging and re-nudging just to “hover” in the right spot.

And that’s not the end — or even the worst — of it. Often times, there’s more than one star system nearby that must be nudged to, since there wasn’t the convenience of a mass relay to get there directly. This is basically a third view that’s similar to the individual star system view, except much, much harder to read. Other star systems are points of light on an already incredibly bright celestial texture background. I suppose it wouldn’t be proper space opera without some fractal-addict’s glowy nebula wallpaper. But when it makes things harder to read…?

So you get around this view with the same toy nudging. Except, here, on a much larger cosmological scale (though, not as large by a long shot as the galactic view where you navigate with a vastly simpler cursor), you naturally have to accelerate your toy to a much higher speed. So, your little toy accelerates and decelerates much slower than in the smaller scale system view that came before.

To make things more complicated, your toy consumes fuel in this view. Traveling between stars costs fuel, but not between planets…you know, just because. And thanks to the heftier momentum your toy harbors at this scale, it’s even less precise — in the physics of Mass Effect, it’s easier to miss the gravity well of a freaking star than it is a planet. Also, according to ME physics, you have an actual top speed beyond which you can’t accelerate. And sadly, you can’t cut off your acceleration and just coast the rest of the way, taking full advantage of Newton’s laws and not spending fuel, like all real world spacecraft do. Because, of course, this is a little toy being nudged around on a glowy table.

The cost of fuel is actually trivialized in the game, since money is easy to come by (the economy of end-of-times deserves deserves its own nitpicking). But the fact that the Normandy has a limited capacity of fuel, you have to be judicious with your tabletop nudging. In the end, it’s clearly a poorly thought out mechanic that tacks on inconvenience to an already inconvenient interstellar navigation system.

There are ways to do this whole interface right — I’d very much consider it a solved problem. Take Google Maps, for instance, which lets you *gasp* text search for what you want, or zoom and pan to find what you want. Or, if you really want to reproduce the feel of hurtling between planets in a map view, take a good gander at Star Control II by Toys For Bob, which came out twenty years ago and did it with aplomb.

This was really one of many (an RPG with no inventory view? Why do all npcs, alien or not, look like they’re in their late 20s?) extremely nerdy nitpicks I have about Mass Effect 3 that nicked away at the illusion. Oh, I know it’s pretty obsessive, but for all of the production value, movie-quality television advertising, and massive marketing blitz, it’s also hard to dismiss. I hope the next time Bioware decides to sink a year and millions of dollars into a project, it really makes sure the end result is completely fleshed out.
